## 1. What the Best Broadway Comedy Sites Actually Do

**Book of Mormon** (bookofmormonbroadway.com) — The gold standard for irreverent comedy marketing. Key move: the entire voice of the site is *in character* with the show's tone. Headlines are punchy. Quote stacking: "the funniest show ever" (Entertainment Weekly), "you'll laugh until it hurts" (Rolling Stone). They also built a custom **"Create Your Own Personalized Video"** tool — a shareable, social-first feature that drove enormous word-of-mouth. The lesson: give people something to *do* on the site, not just read.

**Beetlejuice** (beetlejuicebroadway.com) — The copy is doing the work. "Book your seats before they vanish into the underworld." Even the urgency CTA is in character. Dark, high-contrast, graphic. It commits completely to the show's world. Doesn't try to be a generic theatre site.

**Death Becomes Her** (deathbecomesher.com) — Designed by RPM (who also did Moulin Rouge! and Hamilton). High camp, glamour, bold. The branding agency won a design award specifically for committing to the tone — "dark comedy that tips into high camp" — rather than softening it for a general audience.

**SIX** (sixthemusical.com) — Pop concert energy, not theatre energy. The site feels like a stadium show. Bright, bold, the queens are the heroes of every page. Cast photos are massive. The show *is* the cast, so the cast *is* the site.

**The Great Gatsby Broadway** — Used immersive scroll-based animation. As you scroll down, you enter the party. Felt like the 1920s world was pulling you in. Shows what's possible when the design serves the story.

**Key pattern across all of them:** The best comedy show sites don't describe the show — they *are* the show. Voice, colour, energy, and interactivity all signal the tone before a single word is read.

## 2. What Comedy Sites Do That Drama Sites Don't

- **Lead with the laugh, earn the heart.** The funniest line from the show goes in the hero — not the synopsis. The synopsis is for people who are already interested.
- **Cast as characters, not credits.** The best comedy sites show the cast *performing*, not posing. Action photos beat headshots. Energy beats polish.
- **Colour does the tonal work.** Dark drama = dark sites. Comedies that try dark prestige sites look confused. The colour is the first signal — it tells you before you read a word whether this is fun.
- **Short, punchy copy in the voice of the show.** Not "a sharp-witted, irreverent comedy about…" — that's for the press release. The site copy should sound like the show sounds.
- **Social-forward interactive moment.** Every breakout comedy site has something shareable — a generator, a quiz, a video tool, something that makes the show *spread*.

## 3. Lottery / Game Show / Plinko Visual Language

The aesthetic of scratch tickets, Plinko boards, and game show graphics is very specific and very recognisable:

- **Scratch cards:** Metallic silver/gold scratchable zones. Grid layouts. "YOU WIN" banners. Stars, coins, horseshoes. Bold serif or condensed sans fonts. Black outlines around everything. The feel is: mass-market, slightly trashy in the best way, pure dopamine.
- **Plinko boards:** Dot grids. Primary colours. Stacked vertical movement. The visual rhythm of things *falling* and landing.
- **Game show aesthetics:** Flashing banners. Ticker tape. Everything is a *reveal*. Anticipation baked into the visual language.
- **The key tension for this site:** These aesthetics are deliberately low-brow. The skill is using them with enough wit and craft that they read as *intentional irony* rather than actual cheapness. Beetlejuice pulled this off. So did Book of Mormon. The design has to be doing it *on purpose* — which means the execution has to be impeccable even if the reference is trashy.

**Theatrical shows that have leaned into game show/lottery aesthetics:**
- *The Price Is Right Live* (touring show) — full game show staging, pure spectacle
- No major straight plays have gone full Plinko/lottery — **this is the gap**. Jackpot Twins could own this space entirely.

## 4. Creative Ideas for This Site

1. **Scratch-to-reveal hero section.** On first load, the poster is "scratched" away by the cursor (or auto-animates like a scratch card reveal) to unveil the title and cast. Immediately signals the theme, completely unique, deeply shareable.

2. **The ticker is the personality.** The current yellow ticker is good — but it could do more. Make it interactive: hover any item in the ticker and it expands with a quick joke or fact. "SEANA McKENNA ★ — [hover] → ORDER OF CANADA RECIPIENT. FIRST FEMALE RICHARD III. NOW LOTTERY WINNER."

3. **"What are your odds?" generator.** A simple fun tool: enter a few details about yourself, get a fake lottery probability. "The odds of seeing a better comedy this year: 1 in 1.226 quintillion." Shareable, brandable, costs nothing to build.

4. **Plinko ball that falls down the page as you scroll.** A visual ball/chip animates down through the page sections as you scroll, landing in each section. Plays on the Plinko board reference, makes the scroll feel like the game.

5. **Cast cards flip on click to reveal bio.** Front: headshot with name. Back: bio. Like a lottery card reveal. Already have the bio content. The flip animation reinforces the scratch/reveal theme. *(Note: Dev has already built click-to-open modal — this could be upgraded to a flip animation instead.)*

6. **The quote strip is a jackpot moment.** The pink/dot-pattern section already has Plinko energy. Lean into it further — make the quote animate in like a slot machine landing on the right numbers.

7. **Ticket section styled as a real lottery ticket.** Style the performance dates/info to look exactly like a scratch lottery ticket — perforated edges, ticket number, barcode, "VOID IF DETACHED." Fun, on-brand, completely different from any theatre site.

8. **Sound design.** A very subtle game show "ding" or slot machine sound on key interactions (opening modal, hovering cast). Optional/mutable. Makes the site feel alive.

---

## 5. What to Avoid

- **Dark prestige aesthetic** — every time the site looks like a serious drama, it's actively misleading the audience about what they're buying.
- **Generic theatre copy** — "join us for an evening of…", "a timely and poignant…", "don't miss this…" — sounds like every other show. Jackpot Twins has a specific, weird, funny voice. The site should too.
- **Slow loading** — heavy animation on mobile kills performance. Every effect needs to be lightweight.
- **Burying the cast.** These five actors are the sell. Seana McKenna and Nora McLellan's faces need to be high and prominent.
- **Forgetting mobile.** At least 60% of traffic will be mobile. Ticker, Plinko effects, cast grid — all need mobile equivalents.
- **"Coming Soon" as the hero** after March 31. Once it goes live, everything needs to be there.

---

## Top 5 Recommendations

1. **Scratch-to-reveal animation on the hero.** The single most memorable thing the site could do. Unique, on-brand, shareable.
2. **Style the tickets section as a real lottery ticket** — perforated edge, ticket number, barcode. No other theatre site has done this.
3. **Cast cards flip on click to reveal bio.** Reinforce the scratch/reveal motif across every interactive moment.
4. **Add a "What are your odds?" shareable generator.** Cheap to build, big social upside.
5. **Make the copy sound like the show, not like a theatre.** Every headline and CTA needs Philip's voice — irreverent, confident, funny. e.g. "Get Tickets at Mirvish" → "Claim Your Ticket" or "Enter the Draw."
